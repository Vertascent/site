The Babylonian Method for Square Roots

Published June 1, 2025

The Babylonian method (also known as Heronâ€™s method) is an ancient algorithm for estimating square roots. Given a number \( S \), you start with a guess \( x_0 \), and then iterate using:

\( x_{n+1} = \frac{1}{2} \left( x_n + \frac{S}{x_n} \right) \)

For example, to approximate \( \sqrt{2} \), we might start with \( x_0 = 1 \). Applying the formula:

  • \( x_1 = \frac{1}{2} (1 + \frac{2}{1}) = 1.5 \)
  • \( x_2 = \frac{1}{2} (1.5 + \frac{2}{1.5}) = 1.416\overline{6} \)
  • \( x_3 = \frac{1}{2} (1.4166 + \frac{2}{1.4166}) \approx 1.4142 \)

This converges incredibly fast. In fact, after just a few iterations, the result is accurate to many decimal places.

This method is essentially Newton's method applied to the function \( f(x) = x^2 - S \).
