The Babylonian method (also known as Heron's method) is a fast way to approximate the square root of a number \( n \). It works by iterating the formula:

\( x_{k+1} = \frac{1}{2} \left( x_k + \frac{n}{x_k} \right) \)

Starting with an initial guess \( x_0 \), this sequence converges to \( \sqrt{n} \). For example, let's approximate \( \sqrt{10} \) starting with \( x_0 = 3 \):

  1. \( x_1 = \frac{1}{2} (3 + \frac{10}{3}) = \frac{1}{2} (3 + 3.\overline{3}) = 3.1666\ldots \)
  2. \( x_2 = \frac{1}{2} (3.1666 + \frac{10}{3.1666}) \approx 3.1623 \)

This method is efficient and often used in computer algorithms due to its rapid convergence.
